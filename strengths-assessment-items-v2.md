# Strengths Assessment — Item Set (Working Draft v2)

## What changed from v1

Two challenger lenses drove this revision.

The honesty lens showed that the v1 competence items asked people to judge themselves ("I'm good at X"), which is the easiest kind of item to inflate. Every competence item is now phrased as a claim about the world rather than a self-image, using one of two patterns. Track-record phrasing anchors to outcomes ("the things I take on get finished, not just started"). Social-proof phrasing anchors to how others treat the person ("people bring me the problems that have everyone else stuck"). Social proof is used only where the behavior would already have shown up in ordinary work, so prospective leaders without a formal track record can still answer honestly. Energy items remain the spine of the instrument, because questions about what pulls and drains you have no approved answer and are the hardest to game.

The discriminant lens showed that several v1 sub-strengths were the same thing measured twice, which makes a profile look more precise than it is. Four merges were made: Persuasion and Rallying became Mobilizing, Organizing absorbed Structure, Follow-through absorbed Drive, and Developing others absorbed Supporting. The count went from twenty sub-strengths to sixteen, and the Likert section from forty items to thirty-two.

## How this is built

Four dimensions, framed as a light arc of contribution rather than personality types:

- **Thinking** — generate and judge (5 sub-strengths)
- **Influence** — align and rally (4 sub-strengths)
- **Execution** — drive and finish (3 sub-strengths)
- **Relating** — the relational layer running underneath all three (4 sub-strengths)

Each sub-strength gets two Likert items: one about **competence** (evidence that they do this well) and one about **energy** (does this pull them and light them up). The gap between the two is the point. Someone can score high on competence and low on energy, which tells you they're capable at something that quietly costs them. That "capable but draining" signal is what makes this useful for a right-seat conversation, and it's the individual-level version of the regenerative idea that energy should renew rather than deplete.

Every item is worded toward the strength, never probing for a deficit. This follows the Poetic principle: what you study grows, so the items shouldn't grow attention on shortcomings. A low score is read as configuration data (where this person should and shouldn't spend energy), not as a fault to fix.

On top of the sixteen sub-strengths sits a small **orientation layer**. The sub-strengths measure the "what," meaning the raw capabilities and the energy behind them. The orientation layer measures the "how," meaning whether a person prefers to apply a strength directly themselves or by drawing it out of the team. This maps onto the belief that AiMS is defined not by what leaders do but how they do it. Facilitation is not a strength sitting alongside the others; it is a mode of applying whatever strengths you have, which is why it belongs on its own axis rather than reworded into every item. Keeping the two layers separate lets the instrument measure solo energy honestly and facilitative preference separately, so the distance between them becomes the signal rather than getting hidden.

**Scale:** five points, Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree.

**Wording note for the leader / prospective-leader audience:** items capture the instinct and the pull even for someone who hasn't yet held a leadership seat. They don't assume a current title or position.

## Thinking

*Generate and judge. Ideas, sense-making, and the judgment to know which ideas hold up.*

**Ideation**
- The new ideas I put forward often end up shaping what actually gets done. *(competence, track record)*
- I get a charge out of dreaming up new ideas, even when no one asked for them. *(energy)*

**Problem solving**
- The hard problems I take on tend to actually get solved, not just worked on. *(competence, track record)*
- A knotty problem pulls me in rather than wearing me down. *(energy)*

**Analysis**
- Others often rely on my read of the information to understand what's really going on. *(competence, social proof)*
- I enjoy digging into detail and data to work out what's really happening. *(energy)*

**Foresight**
- When I've called where things were heading, I've mostly turned out to be right. *(competence, track record)*
- Thinking through what's coming next for the business is work I'd choose to do. *(energy)*

**Judgment**
- Looking back at the calls I've made on which ideas to back, they've mostly held up. *(competence, track record)*
- I like being the one who sizes up whether an idea will hold up. *(energy)*

---

## Influence

*Align and rally. Bringing people around, mobilizing a group, and providing direction.*

**Mobilizing** *(merged from Persuasion and Rallying: winning people over, one person or a whole room)*
- When something needs buy-in, I'm usually able to get people genuinely behind it. *(competence, track record)*
- I come alive when I'm winning people over to something I believe in. *(energy)*

**Communication**
- When something complex needs explaining to a group, I'm often the one asked to do it. *(competence, social proof)*
- I look for chances to be the one who frames the message or speaks to the room. *(energy)*

**Direction**
- When a decision was stalling, I've been able to step in and get the group to a call. *(competence, track record)*
- When a group I'm part of lacks direction, I feel a pull to provide it. *(energy)*

**Connecting**
- New people tend to warm to me quickly. *(competence, social proof)*
- Meeting and drawing in people I don't yet know is something I actively enjoy. *(energy)*

---

## Execution

*Drive and finish. Follow-through, organization, and dependable ownership.*

**Follow-through** *(absorbs Drive: finishing what's started, with the stamina to sustain it)*
- The things I take on get finished, not just started. *(competence, track record)*
- I can't rest easy until something I've started is actually done. *(energy)*

**Organizing** *(absorbs Structure: coordinating the work and building the routines that keep it running)*
- Work I've organized tends to run smoothly and keep running after I step back. *(competence, track record)*
- I enjoy bringing order to something scattered and building the routine that keeps it that way. *(energy)*

**Ownership**
- I'm the person people hand something to when it absolutely has to get done. *(competence, social proof)*
- I feel a pull to be the person others can count on to get it done. *(energy)*

---

## Relating

*The relational layer beneath the other three. Developing, understanding, and connecting with people.*

**Developing others** *(absorbs Supporting: helping people succeed and grow)*
- People seek me out for help getting better at what they do. *(competence, social proof)*
- Helping someone develop is some of the most rewarding work I do. *(energy)*

**Empathy**
- People tend to feel understood by me, sometimes before they've fully explained themselves. *(competence, social proof)*
- I'm naturally drawn to paying attention to what the people around me need. *(energy)*

**Building trust**
- The close working relationships I've built have lasted and held up under pressure. *(competence, track record)*
- Investing in a close working relationship is something I find genuinely satisfying. *(energy)*

**Including**
- People who might otherwise sit at the edges of a group tend to get drawn in when I'm involved. *(competence, social proof)*
- I feel a pull to make sure everyone in a group is brought in. *(energy)*

## Orientation layer (the "how")

The orientation layer measures how a person prefers to apply their strengths, along a spectrum from direct to facilitative. Direct means working the strength yourself and bringing the result. Facilitative means drawing the result out of the team.

**Scope.** Orientation applies cleanly to Thinking and Execution, applies in a reframed way to Influence, and does not apply to Relating. Thinking and Execution are the clean cases: you can solve a problem yourself or draw the solution out of the group, and you can drive the work yourself or build the team's ability to drive it. Influence is subtler, because it already involves other people, so the axis there is influencing people yourself versus creating the conditions for a group to move itself. Relating is out, because relating is expressed through and toward others by definition, so a direct-versus-facilitative split collapses there and measures nothing.

**Output for v1 of the product.** Produce one overall orientation reading, a lean along the direct-to-facilitative spectrum, drawn from the six items below. Each item is tagged by its dimension in the data, so a later version can report orientation per dimension if results show that's worth it, with no rework.

**Response format.** Each item is a forced choice with intensity: strongly A, lean A, lean B, or strongly B. There is no neutral midpoint, so the person reveals a preference rather than sitting on the fence. Both options are framed as energy or preference rather than capability, so neither reads as the more capable answer.

**Anti-gaming measure.** In the app, the direct and facilitative options should swap sides at random from item to item, so the facilitative choice is not always B. This is the main defense against a respondent who knows the AiMS model and simply picks the approved column all the way down.

**Item 1 — Thinking (problem solving)**
- A. I get more energy from working a hard problem through myself and arriving at a solution.
- B. I get more energy from getting the right people together and working the problem through as a group.

**Item 2 — Thinking (judgment)**
- A. When there's a call to make on which idea to back, I'd rather form my own view and put it forward.
- B. When there's a call to make on which idea to back, I'd rather draw out the group's thinking and help them land it.

**Item 3 — Execution (follow-through)**
- A. I feel most productive when I'm the one doing the work and pushing it to done.
- B. I feel most productive when I'm setting others up to do the work and keeping it moving.

**Item 4 — Execution (organizing)**
- A. I'd rather personally organize the pieces so a project runs the way it should.
- B. I'd rather build the routines that let the team keep a project running without me.

**Item 5 — Influence (mobilizing)**
- A. To move something forward, I'd rather make the case myself and win people over.
- B. To move something forward, I'd rather create the conditions for the group to talk itself into it.

**Item 6 — Influence (direction)**
- A. When a group needs direction, I get energy from being the one who provides it.
- B. When a group needs direction, I get energy from helping the group find its own.

**How it reads with the sub-strengths.** Hold the orientation lean next to the energy scores from the sixteen sub-strengths. The signal you most want to catch is a person with high solo energy on a strength, a strongly direct orientation, and a seat that needs the facilitative version. That combination is the coaching conversation, now visible and nameable rather than hidden inside a virtuous item. For development use, it reads as understanding about how the person prefers to work. For right-seat use, it reads as configuration, meaning the fit between how they apply their strengths and how the seat needs them applied.

## The narrative side

The Likert items above do the measuring. They cover all sixteen sub-strengths. The narrative step is not there to measure the same ground again, so it doesn't need many questions. Its job is to confirm the Likert picture, add texture, and surface the places where someone's self-rating and their lived experience diverge.

**Format:** one open best-self prompt to start, then one or two adaptive follow-ups the model asks based on what the person actually said, the way a coach would in an appreciative interview. Two or three exchanges total, not a list of essays. This keeps the step short, keeps it from draining people, and stays true to the inquiry-creates-change belief. The narrative step can also be made optional; the assessment still works on the Likert items alone, with less confirmation.

**Opening prompt (use one):**
- Think of a time at work when you were at your best, a moment you'd point to and say that's when I was really in my element. What was happening, what were you doing, and what made it feel that way?
- Describe a recent stretch of work that gave you energy rather than draining it, where you lost track of time. What were you actually doing?

**Why the prompts are not labeled by dimension:** naming the dimension in the question would telegraph the answer and narrow it. The person tells an open story, and the model codes whatever surfaces onto the sixteen sub-strengths. Follow-ups can be lightly targeted once there's a signal worth confirming, since at that point you're confirming rather than fishing.

**Honesty note:** the narrative side is also a quiet honesty check. Stories about specific past behavior are harder to inflate than agreement with a general statement, so where a story contradicts a high self-rating, weight the story.

## Reading the two sides together

**Competence high, energy low** on a sub-strength: the person can do it but it costs them. This is the configuration signal for right-seat conversations. It's information about where to spend their energy, not a mark against them.

**Story aligns with high energy scores** on the same sub-strengths: a confident read. The self-rating and the lived experience agree.

**Story and scores diverge:** that gap is the coaching conversation. It's often the most interesting thing in the profile.

**Worked example (Influence):** if someone agrees strongly with the Direction competence item but disagrees with the Direction energy item, they can set direction but it drains them. If their best-self story is about steadying a shaken team through a hard quarter, that codes onto Mobilizing and Direction here, and probably Building trust over in Relating. When the story lines up with high energy on those same sub-strengths, the read is confident. Where it diverges, that's where the coaching happens.

---

## Two framings of the same data, by use

- **Coaching and development:** lean fully into inquiry for understanding. The profile is something the person helps surface and confirm, not a verdict handed to them.
- **Right-seat fit:** frame it as configuration, meaning the fit between a person's energy and the demands of the work, rather than a judgment of the person. Same underlying data, deliberately different framing. Treat the result as a current snapshot that informs development and role shaping, since roles evolve and capability develops, not as a permanent match score.

## Known limits and pairs to watch

- This is a self-report instrument, so it measures self-perception, not observed behavior. The track-record and social-proof phrasing raises the honesty floor but doesn't remove the ceiling. A later credibility step would be light observer input, in the spirit of the Reflected Best Self method.
- Track-record and social-proof items can pick up a modesty bias, where genuinely strong but self-effacing people underclaim. The energy items and the narrative side are the counterweights, and divergence between them and the competence items should be read with modesty in mind, not just inflation.
- **Pairs to watch empirically once there's response data:** Analysis and Problem solving (shared cognitive core), Judgment against the rest of Thinking (risk of dissolving into Analysis plus Foresight), and the merged strengths (confirm Mobilizing, Organizing, Follow-through, and Developing others each behave as one coherent strength rather than two forced together).
- If a merged pair turns out to separate on energy (equal competence, different pull), that's evidence to re-split it. The merges are provisional and the data model should keep the old sub-tags so a re-split costs nothing.

## Open items for next iterations

- Whether to reverse-word a small number of items to guard against straight-line responding, and how to do that without introducing deficit framing.
- How the individual profile rolls up to a team view, so strengths can be configured across people rather than left competing.
- Whether to expand orientation from one overall reading to a per-dimension reading, once results show whether the finer view earns its complexity. The item tagging already supports this without rework.
- Pressure-testing the orientation item wording with real respondents, to confirm neither side reads as the approved answer in practice.
